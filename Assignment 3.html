Unconventional Monetary Policies and Inequality in the EU¶

Riccardo Filesi 460856

Research Question¶

Have the post-crisis unconventional monetary policies implemented by the ECB - namely SMP, LTROs, OMT and QE - increased income inequalities in the EU?

Motivation¶

“Within our mandate, the ECB is ready to do whatever it takes to preserve the euro. And believe me, it will be enough.” - Mario Draghi, UKTI's Global Investment Conference (26th July 2012)

The ECB is one of the main players in the Eurosystem. During the last financial crisis, it had the hard task to preserve the existence of a single currency in the Eurozone, relying in some cases on extraordinary monetary policies. Since the Euro is still in use, we can assume that such instruments have been successful in reaching their goal. But we may further study at what price the Euro has been preserved. In this sense, it is interesting to analyse the redistributive effects of unconventional monetary policies implemented over the period 2010-2015: how have they affected income differences among the EU population?

Method¶

I use a multiple regression model where the GINI index is measure of income inequality per country i per year t - a Gini coefficient of zero expresses perfect equality, where everyone has the same income. My variable of interest is $UMP_t$: this is a dummy variable that assumes value 1 if in a particular year the ECB implemented an unconventional monetary policy and 0 otherwise. Hence, I first run the following regression, including a first and second lag of the dependent variable to solve the likely presence of autocorrelation:

$$ Gini_{i,t} = \alpha + \beta_0 UMP_t + \beta_1 Gini_{i,t-1} + \beta_2 Gini_{i,t-2} + \varepsilon_t .$$

In the Robustness section, I will further extend my model.

The dataset is available at The World Bank and OECD.

Answer¶

As the results of my regression show, the presence of unconventional monetary policies reduced inequality in the Eurozone. Anyway, including additional variables that are good predictors of income inequality leds to opposite results: unconventional monetary policies raised inequality over the relevant period. I will provide a possible explaination in the Discussion.

Main Assumptions¶

Since I use multiple regression models, I assume that there is a a linear relationship between the outcome variable and the independent variables and that the residuals are normally distributed. Then, I presume the presence of no-multicollinearity, so that the independent variables are not highly correlated with each other. Finally, I assume the presence of homoschedasticity, meaning that the variance of error terms are similar across the values of the independent variables.

Moreover, though not implemented, OMT has never been terminated: hence its announcement had effects from 2014 forward. This is why I assume the presence of unconventional monetary policies also in 2015.

Unconventional policies¶

When a nation's economy grows to the point that inflation increases to dangerous levels, the central bank will pursue restrictive monetary policy to tighten the money supply and reduce the amount of money in circulation. When a nation's economy slips into recession, this policy tool can be operated in reverse: interest rates are lowered, reserve limits loosened and bonds are purchased in exchange for newly created money.

The issue with such conventional policies is that in periods of deep recession they become limited. According to RBI’s Deepak Mohanty, "When central banks look beyond their traditional instrument of policy interest rate, monetary policy takes an unconventional character." In my analysis I will focus on four unconventional monetary policies implemented by the ECB as a response to the last financial crisis: SMP, LTROs, OMT and QE.

Securities Markets Programme (SMP)¶

The “Securities Markets Programme” (SMP), announced on 10 May 2010, is intended to ensure depth and liquidity in malfunctioning segments of the debt securities markets and to restore an appropriate functioning of the monetary policy transmission mechanism; the objective is to restore the effective conduct of monetary policy oriented towards price stability in the medium term. The impact of these interventions is sterilised offering commercial banks the possibility to invest central bank money at the ECB as a time deposit: through such specific operations, the liquidity injected is re-absorbed and thereby the monetary policy stance is not affected. The SMP simply involved the purchase of bonds – especially sovereign bonds – on the secondary markets. Owing to the ban on monetary financing, no purchases were made on the primary market, and the last purchase was made in February 2012. At its peak, the programme's volume totalled around €210 billion.

Source: blogs.reuters.com

Outright Monetary Transactions (OMT)¶

On 2 August 2012, the Governing Council of the European Central Bank (ECB) announced that it would undertake outright transactions in secondary, sovereign bond markets, aimed at safeguarding an appropriate monetary policy transmission and the singleness of the monetary policy. The technical framework of these operations was formulated on 6 September 2012. On the same date, the bank's SMP was terminated. For the OMT to be activated towards a certain eurozone state, a total of four conditions needs to be fully met:

  1. Conditionality: The state needs to have received financial sovereign support from the eurozone's bailout funds EFSF/ESM.
  2. Coverage: No ex ante quantitative limits are set on the size of Outright Monetary Transactions.
  3. Creditor treatment: The Eurosystem clarifies in the legal act concerning Outright Monetary Transactions that it accepts the same (pari passu) treatment as private or other creditors with respect to bonds issued by euro area countries and purchased by the Eurosystem through Outright Monetary Transactions, in accordance with the terms of such bonds.
  4. Sterilization: The liquidity created through Outright Monetary Transactions will be fully sterilised.

The OMT has never been implemented. Its only announcement, by the way, has had positive effects in reducing bond yields.

Source: BusinessInsider.com

Longer-Term Refinancing Operations (LTROs)¶

LTROs provide an injection of low interest rate funding to euro zone banks with sovereign debt as collateral on the loans. Until early 2008, the longest LTRO maturity was three months. Since then the ECB has successively introduced six-month, 12-month and 36-month terms for LTRO finance. Each of these new issues has been heavily subscribed, with eurozone periphery banks in Ireland, Italy, Spain and Greece taking the majority of the first 36-month issue in late 2011. The second 36-month issue in February 2012 was closely watched as an indicator of the health of eurozone banks. Since early 2008, the loans are offered monthly and are typically repaid in three months, six months or one year. The second 36-month issue in February 2012 was closely watched as an indicator of the health of eurozone banks. The goal of such policy is twofold: encouraging euro zone banks to increase lending activities that spur the economy by providing access to cheap capital and lowering sovereign debt yields since countries can use their own sovereign bonds as collateral, with a consequent increase in their demand.

Source: NCBs, Goldman Sachs Research

Quantitative Easing (QE)¶

The ECB started buying assets from commercial banks in March 2015 as part of its non-standard monetary policy measures. These asset purchases, also known as QE, support economic growth across the euro area and help to return to inflation levels below, but close to, 2%. By buying eurozone governments’ bonds using electronically created money, the ECB hoped to shore up confidence, boost inflation, and drive down the value of the euro on foreign exchanges, helping to boost exports and kickstart growth. The €60bn a month used includes the purchases of private sector assets to try to unlock credit markets. Mario Draghi announced that he would pump €1.1tn at a rate of €60bn (£46bn) a month into financial markets until September 2016, in an attempt to prevent the fragile eurozone economy from grinding to a halt.

Results¶

As we can see from the sign of the coefficient of $UMP_t$ in the regression, the presence of unconventional policies had a negative impact on the Gini index. Since a lower Gini index means a lower inequality, there is evidence of a positive redistribution of incomes. This must not be confused, though, as a general increase of wealth: for instance, inequality may be decreased as a result of lower high incomes.

Robustness¶

To check the robustness of my results I try to extend my model. The findings of Jaumotte et al. (2008) suggest that the most significant covariates for explaining change in income inequality are the credit to the private sector, the ratio of inward FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) to GDP and the industrial employment share. The first two variables have a positive correlation with income inequality: that is, an increase in one of the two causes an increase in Gini, while industrial employment share is negatively associated with Gini. Furthermore, the export-to-GDP ratio is significantly negatively correlated with Gini, which suggests that trade globalization is associated with lower income inequality (ibid.). Thus I run now the following regression, taking into account inflation: $$ Gini_{i,t} = \alpha + \beta_0 UMP_t + \beta_1 Gini_{i,t-1} + \beta_2 Gini_{i,t-2} + \beta_3 \frac{Credit_{i,t}}{GDP_{i,t}} + \beta_4 \frac{FDI_{i,t}}{GDP_{i,t}} + \beta_5 Industrial_{{i,t}} + \beta_6 \frac{Export_{i,t}}{GDP_{i,t}} + \beta_7 \Pi_{i,t} + \varepsilon_t .$$

From what we see, the presence of unconventional monetary policy raised inequality. The coefficient of my variable of interest - $UMP_t$ - is indeed positive.

Discussion¶

The opposite results may be due to the fact that in the second model I included more variables which are high-effective predictors of income inequality. Here, the results make more sense if we consider how unconventional policies work: they simply provide the creation of wealth through injection of money in the markets or in the banking system. Usually, the users of these channels already own a high level of wealth, and as a result they become even richer if compared to the poor. Of course, over the years, such increase of richness will be redistributed to the lower income-classes for example through higher consumptions, but this requires time. To date, maybe the policies are so recent that, in the second and probably more reliable regression I have run, we can not observe positive redistributive effects.

Final Considerations¶

One of the main weakness of my research is the lack of data following the introduction of QE in 2015. So I may underestimate or not estimate at all the effects of such policy. This is an issue to address in future analysis considering, the impact that QE had on the eurozone economy and its monetary amount.

Furthermore, the econometric model I designed could be surely improved. The use of a dummy variable to test for the effects of the unconventional monetary policies is probably not precise enough. The best strategy would be to create different variables for each policy, using country- and time-specific data for the quantity of money that injected. Here, again, the problem I faced was a lack of data, since many financial institutions did not share how much money they borrowed, as such amount could be a signal of financial distress.
